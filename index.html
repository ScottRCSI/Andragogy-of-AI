The Andragogy
of AI

How adult learning theory explains and accelerates genuine AI adoption in the workplace.

Central thesis

"Your expertise is the point. AI just extends its reach."

Overview

The problem with how we talk about AI at work

Most organizational AI adoption frameworks treat the workforce as a technology deployment problem: train people, manage change, measure compliance. This fundamentally misunderstands both AI and the adults using it.

Malcolm Knowles' andragogy, the theory of adult learning, offers a more useful lens. Adults bring deep reservoirs of prior experience, strong self-concepts, and practical motivation. They need to know why before they will engage with how.

This research argues that effective AI adoption is not a training problem. It is a learning design problem, with meaningful implications for how organizations communicate, frame, and integrate AI into professional practice.

Central Proposition

The same AI capability can either threaten professional identity or extend expert reach, depending entirely on how it is framed. That framing is not cosmetic. It is the mechanism of adoption.

Theoretical Framework

Six principles, reinterpreted for the AI era

Knowles identified six core assumptions about adult learners. Each maps directly to observable patterns in how professionals engage with, or resist, AI tools in workplace contexts.

1

Self-Concept

Adults move from dependency toward self-direction. AI adoption succeeds when it reinforces professional autonomy, not when it positions workers as AI operators or prompt technicians.

Identity framing
2

Prior Experience

Accumulated expertise is the resource, not the obstacle. Effective AI integration treats domain knowledge as the irreplaceable input that gives AI outputs meaning and direction.

Knowledge architecture
3

Readiness to Learn

Adults engage when learning solves a real problem they currently face. AI framed around abstract future capability stalls. AI framed around today's friction accelerates adoption.

Motivation design
4

Orientation to Learning

Problem-centered, not subject-centered. Adults want to apply, not accumulate. Training organized around AI features fails where programs organized around use cases succeed.

Program design
5

Internal Motivation

Durable AI adoption is driven by intrinsic value: better work, faster thinking, reduced cognitive load. Not by organizational mandate or competitive fear.

Adoption dynamics
6

Need to Know

Adults require a compelling rationale before investing effort. Organizations that deploy AI without answering "what does this mean for my work" encounter predictable resistance.

Change communication

Key Concept

The Identity Subtext Audit

One of the most consequential and least-discussed dimensions of AI communication is identity subtext: the implicit signal embedded in how a capability is named, framed, and positioned.

Consider a single capability: AI-generated first drafts. The same feature can be presented as a cost-reduction measure, a compliance tool, or an expert amplifier. Each framing is factually accurate. Each triggers a different psychological and behavioral response in adult professionals.

Core Insight

Before any AI communication is finalized, it is worth asking: what does this framing say about the value of the person receiving it? That question, the identity subtext audit, may be the highest-leverage intervention in AI adoption strategy.

This concept has direct applications in organizational change management, internal communications strategy, and the design of AI-integrated workflows. It is especially relevant in knowledge-intensive professions where expert identity is tightly coupled to professional value.

Research Scope & Domains

Where this framework applies

The Andragogy of AI framework is domain-agnostic in principle, but initially grounded in three applied contexts where adult learner characteristics and AI capability most acutely intersect.

Domain I Enterprise & Professional Organizations

How do knowledge workers in large organizations navigate AI integration when their professional identity, authority, and compensation are tied to expertise that AI appears to replicate? Change communication, leadership framing, and role architecture are examined through the andragogical lens.

Domain II Healthcare & Clinical Practice

Clinicians represent a high-stakes case: deep expertise, high autonomy, strong professional identity, and significant resistance to perceived de-skilling. AI in diagnostic support, documentation, and patient communication surfaces each of the six andragogical tensions simultaneously.

Domain III Higher Education & Curriculum

How should educators, themselves adult learners, redesign curriculum when AI is available to students? This domain explores AI not as a cheating problem to be managed, but as a pedagogical design challenge that andragogy is well-positioned to address.

Research Status

This work is ongoing

The Andragogy of AI is an active research project. The theoretical framework presented here represents the current working structure, which continues to evolve as the literature review deepens and applied case material is gathered.

Anticipated outputs include a peer-reviewed journal article, practitioner-facing white paper, and potentially a short-form book expanding the framework for general professional audiences. Collaborative development is in consideration.
